Choosing a Legal Form

Which Business Form to Choose in Cyprus

Cyprus law allows for several types of business structures. For most international companies, IT, e-commerce, and holding structures, the optimal choice is a Private Limited Company (Ltd).

Recommended

Ltd — Private Company Limited by Shares

The primary form for international business. Shareholder liability is limited to their share in the capital. Suitable for IT, e-commerce, consulting, and holding structures. The recommended option in 95% of cases.

PLC — Public Company Limited by Shares

For larger structures with the ability to raise capital publicly. Requires a minimum of 7 shareholders, 2 directors, and a separate company secretary.

Partnership / Joint Venture

A structure for joint projects between several participants. Profit and liability are distributed among partners according to the partnership agreement.

Branch of a Foreign Company

Allows a presence in Cyprus without establishing a separate legal entity. Suitable for companies that want a local presence while retaining the parent company's structure.

Sole Trader

A simplified status for local activity by a single individual. Does not create a separate legal entity, and liability is unlimited.

Licensed activities in Cyprus

For most businesses — IT, consulting, trading, holding structures — registering an Ltd with the Cyprus Registrar of Companies is sufficient to start operating. Certain activities require a separate regulatory licence:

  • Investment services, forex and CFDs (Cyprus Investment Firm, CIF) — CySEC licence, Law 87(I)/2017
  • Banking — Central Bank of Cyprus (CBC) licence
  • Insurance — Insurance Companies Control Service (ICCS) licence, Ministry of Finance Cyprus
  • Payment institutions and electronic money (Payment Institution / EMI) — CBC licence (PSD2 / EMD2)
  • Pharmaceuticals — authorisation from the Pharmaceutical Services, Ministry of Health Cyprus

Obtaining a regulatory licence is a separate process and is not part of standard company incorporation. If your activity falls into one of the categories above, let us know at the consultation stage — we will assess the regulatory requirements and recommend a corporate structure suited to the licensing process.

Company Registration in Cyprus: the complete 2026 guide

Why open a company in Cyprus — not just a tax question

Choosing a jurisdiction is a long-term decision. The question is not simply where the tax rate is lowest — it is where your company can operate without legal risk, access banking infrastructure without friction, and present a credible corporate identity to clients, investors and counterparties for the next decade.

Cyprus occupies a unique position: it is not an offshore centre, nor a typical grey-list jurisdiction, yet it offers one of the most competitive tax systems in the EU. As of 1 January 2026, the standard corporate tax rate has been raised from 12.5% to 15% as part of a tax reform aligning Cyprus with the OECD global minimum tax — even after the increase, Cyprus remains one of the most competitive jurisdictions in Europe for corporate taxation. For intellectual property income, the IP Box regime still exempts 80% of such profit from tax, while the remaining 20% is taxed at the standard rate — an effective rate of approximately 3%.

What distinguishes Cyprus from offshore alternatives is the combination of legitimate EU status and competitive taxation. A Cyprus company carries an EU VAT number, operates under EU law and is accepted by counterparties in London, New York, Dubai and Singapore — without the due diligence complications typically associated with offshore structures. In practice, this means open access to European banking infrastructure: Bank of Cyprus, Eurobank, Alpha Bank Cyprus and other licensed Cypriot banks work with Cyprus companies, alongside international electronic money institutions such as Wise Business, Revolut Business and Airwallex.

Finally, Cyprus is one of the few jurisdictions where company registration provides a direct route to a residence permit for the director. This makes a Cyprus structure a tool not only for tax planning, but for personal planning as well.

Important: as of 1 January 2026, Cyprus's standard corporate tax rate has been raised from 12.5% to 15% for all Cyprus tax residents — this is a separate domestic tax reform measure. Do not confuse it with the OECD Pillar Two global minimum tax, which applies separately to multinational groups with consolidated global revenue above €750 million and is calculated under its own rules, independent of the domestic rate. Despite the increase, Cyprus remains one of the most competitive tax jurisdictions in the EU.

Director and shareholder requirements

Cyprus law imposes no nationality or residency requirements on directors or shareholders. Any individual or legal entity, regardless of nationality, may serve as the sole director and sole shareholder of a Cyprus Ltd.

Minimum requirements:

  • Minimum one director (individual or corporate body)
  • Minimum one shareholder (individual or corporate body)
  • A registered office in Cyprus
  • A company secretary (may be a corporate entity)

The Cyprus Companies Law (Cap. 113, s. 29) limits the number of members of a private company (Ltd) to 50, excluding current and former employees who hold shares. Shareholders may be individuals or legal entities — a foreign company, holding structure or foundation. Where the shareholder is a corporate entity, Cyprus law requires disclosure of the Ultimate Beneficial Owner (UBO): the natural person who ultimately exercises control over the company.

Under Section 171 of the Cyprus Companies Law (Cap. 113), one person can act as director, secretary and shareholder at the same time — but only in a single-member company, where all three roles are held by the same individual. If there are multiple shareholders, or the director and shareholder are different people, the director cannot also serve as secretary: that role must be held by a separate person or entity. In practice, registered office and company secretarial services are usually provided by professional corporate service providers.

Do you need a local Cyprus director?

Not legally required. However, a Cyprus-resident director is generally recommended to establish and maintain Cyprus tax residence.

The concept of the Place of Effective Management (POEM) determines a company's tax residence. If all significant management decisions are made by a director sitting in Germany, the UK or the UAE, the tax authorities of that country may argue that the company is effectively managed from there — and therefore tax-resident there, not in Cyprus. For companies with multiple directors, a similar principle applies: the majority of the board (more than 50%) should be Cyprus residents, with key decisions made on the island — otherwise the company risks failing to establish Cyprus tax residence.

Appointing a Cyprus-resident director with genuine management authority mitigates this risk. At a minimum, this means:

  • Board meetings held physically in Cyprus
  • The resident director signs documents and exercises real decision-making authority
  • Management functions demonstrably performed from Cyprus

For smaller companies, a professional nominee director with documented authority and regular board meetings in Cyprus typically satisfies POEM requirements. Larger operations with significant IP income or complex structures may need more substantial local presence — local staff, office space and active operational management from Cyprus.

Cyprus has concluded 60+ double tax treaties covering most major jurisdictions — UAE, UK, Israel, India, Canada, China and the majority of EU member states. Individuals who relocate to Cyprus as tax residents can benefit from the Non-Domiciled (Non-Dom) status. Under this regime, foreign-sourced dividend income and interest received by a non-dom individual are exempt from Special Defence Contribution (SDC) — a 17-year exemption available to qualifying individuals. This makes Cyprus one of the most attractive personal tax environments in the EU for business owners and investors who choose to live on the island.

Mandatory annual reporting for Cyprus companies

  • Audited Financial Statements — prepared under IFRS and signed off by a licensed Cyprus auditor. Filed with the Tax Department together with the corporation tax return.
  • Corporation Tax Return (IR4) — filed annually with the Cyprus Tax Department. Two provisional tax instalments must be paid during the tax year: by 31 July and by 31 December. Late payment attracts a 5% surcharge plus interest. The exact filing deadline for the final return should be confirmed with your accountant — the Tax Department routinely grants extensions in practice.

Individual companies may also receive statistical questionnaires from the Cyprus Statistical Service (CYSTAT) as part of sample surveys — participation is mandatory if selected, but this is not a universal annual requirement for all companies.

VAT in Cyprus: when and how to register

Cyprus VAT is governed by EU VAT Directive principles. The main registration triggers for a Cyprus company are:

  • Taxable supplies within Cyprus exceed €15,600 per year
  • Intra-EU supplies of goods are made (regardless of value)
  • Digital/electronic services are provided to B2C customers in the EU — the EU-wide €10,000 annual threshold (OSS scheme) applies, below which the seller's home-country VAT rate can be used

For B2B services within the EU, the reverse charge mechanism generally applies — the buyer accounts for VAT in their own country, so the seller typically does not need to register.

Voluntary registration is available from day one — and is typically advisable, as it allows the company to recover input VAT on startup costs such as office rent, equipment and professional fees from the very beginning of trading.

A Cyprus VAT number takes the format CY + 8 digits + 1 letter and can be verified through the VIES system for intra-EU B2B transactions. VAT returns are submitted quarterly; late filing incurs a €51 penalty per return plus interest on unpaid amounts.

Corporate banking for Cyprus companies

Opening a corporate bank account is often the most time-sensitive step after incorporation. Cypriot banks conduct thorough KYC/KYB due diligence and the process can be unexpectedly slow without proper preparation. Understanding the landscape in advance is essential.

Since 2025, Cypriot banks have tightened compliance procedures for clients from certain jurisdictions, including Russia — account opening may take longer or require a more extensive document package. Common mistakes when setting up a Cyprus company

After 350+ incorporations, we have seen the same avoidable mistakes repeatedly. Each one costs time and money. Here are the most common:

No substance — risk of losing Cyprus tax residency

The company is incorporated in Cyprus but all key decisions are made from another country. This can result in the company being treated as a tax resident of another jurisdiction.

Leaving the bank account for later

Without a working bank account the company cannot operate. Bank compliance review and document preparation can take months — start immediately after incorporation.

Ignoring bookkeeping from day one

Cyprus law requires accounting records from the date of incorporation. Reconstructing neglected records is expensive and creates serious problems during the statutory audit.

Wrong ownership structure

Direct personal ownership by an individual from certain jurisdictions creates KYC complications at banks, payment processors and future investors. A proper holding structure prevents these issues.

Missing the Annual Return deadline

Late or non-submission of the Annual Return results in penalties and ultimately in the company being struck off the register.

VAT registration without understanding the obligations

VAT registration means mandatory quarterly filings. Missing deadlines triggers automatic penalties and additional scrutiny.

What to do after your Cyprus company is registered

Receiving the Certificate of Incorporation is the beginning of the process, not the end. A practical post-incorporation checklist for a Cyprus company:

  • Obtain Tax Identification Number (TIN/TIC) from the Cyprus Tax Department
  • Register for VAT (mandatory if required, voluntary if beneficial)
  • Open a corporate bank account
  • Appoint an accountant and establish a bookkeeping system
  • Prepare standard contract templates and invoice formats
  • If required, apply for a Cyprus residence permit for the director
  • File Annual Return on time each year (deadline 31 December)
  • File annual financial statements and corporation tax return
  • If employing staff: register with Social Insurance Services. Work permits are only required for non-EU nationals — EU citizens can work in Cyprus without one under freedom of movement rules.
